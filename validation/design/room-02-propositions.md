# Room 02 — Comparative design propositions

## Why this document exists

The previous Room 02 model was technically navigable but insufficient as a stakeholder visualization. The purpose of this proposition set is not to generate three decorative variants. It is to test three different beliefs about what the room should be.

The source reference imagery is treated as visual evidence, not ground truth. These propositions preserve useful qualities from that evidence while explicitly rejecting generic AI-lab conventions, decorative screens, and centerpiece objects without a functional role.

## Shared requirements

All three propositions retain:

- the production room envelope: **15.2 m × 13.8 m × 5.7 m**;
- six independently editable learner workbench assemblies;
- six independently editable laptop children;
- one coaching table;
- one credential display;
- one Room 02 demonstration asset;
- usable circulation rather than floor-filling composition;
- visible relationships among learning, demonstration, coaching, and credential achievement.

They deliberately differ in hierarchy, circulation, social behavior, and what the stakeholder is meant to remember.

---

## Proposition A — The Academy Axis

### Belief

The room should first be understood as a credible academy. Its strongest value is clarity: instruction leads to practice, practice leads to assessment, and assessment leads to visible credentials.

### Spatial thesis

A disciplined center aisle terminates at the active teaching and demonstration zone. Two workstation rows reinforce the progression. Coaching and credential review sit on opposing sides of the destination.

### What it keeps

- strong longitudinal organization;
- repeated learner rhythm;
- warm institutional material character;
- a clearly shared instructional view.

### What it rejects

- technology used as decoration;
- a floating showcase object in unused floor area;
- workstations whose orientation does not communicate activity;
- circulation that must be inferred rather than seen.

### Intended stakeholder response

> “I immediately understand how instruction happens here, and I can see how a learner progresses toward a credential.”

### Risk

It may feel formal or conventional. The coaching zone is supportive rather than socially central.

---

## Proposition B — The Credential Gallery

### Belief

The room should make achievement public. Visitors should understand the learning journey by moving through it, not by reading a label.

### Spatial thesis

Two learning galleries form inhabited edges around a central promenade. Coaching welcomes people at the threshold. A demonstration occupies the middle of the journey, and the credential wall terminates it.

### What it keeps

- visible evidence of achievement;
- a public-facing demonstration moment;
- a memorable procession;
- clear distinction between learner territory and visitor circulation.

### What it rejects

- classroom rows;
- credentials treated as a secondary display;
- coaching hidden at the back;
- objects distributed without narrative sequence.

### Intended stakeholder response

> “This room tells the story of learning and achievement before anyone explains it to me.”

### Risk

The promenade consumes more floor area. Learners face inward rather than sharing one instructor-facing view.

---

## Proposition C — The Learning Forum

### Belief

The room should express a participatory culture. The primary visual message is not computers or credentials; it is people thinking and making together.

### Spatial thesis

The coaching table becomes the social heart. Six workbench assemblies form an irregular ring around it. Demonstration and credential evidence act as opposing anchors rather than one dominant centerpiece.

### What it keeps

- human-centered collaboration;
- multiple ways to enter and participate;
- visible tools within reach of people;
- balance between experimentation and evidence of achievement.

### What it rejects

- rigid classroom rows;
- a single dominant technology object;
- ceremonial distance between occupants and tools;
- a room that reads as a staged showroom.

### Intended stakeholder response

> “This feels like a place where people work together, not a room arranged for a photograph.”

### Risk

It is less efficient for lecture-style instruction. Its distributed circulation requires more accessibility and sightline review.

---

## Comparative decision

The stakeholder is not being asked to choose a color scheme. The useful questions are:

1. Should the room’s first message be **instruction**, **achievement**, or **participation**?
2. Should movement be organized by one clear axis, one ceremonial promenade, or a distributed forum loop?
3. Should the credential outcome be a supporting zone, the final destination, or one of two balanced anchors?
4. How formal should the learning culture feel?
5. Which proposition contains the strongest idea even if its current blockout is not yet visually resolved?

A valid outcome may combine elements, but the combination must preserve a coherent primary hierarchy. Combining every attractive feature without choosing a dominant belief would recreate the generic composition problem this exercise is meant to solve.

## Review boundary

These are editable blockout propositions. They establish spatial intent, hierarchy, relationships, and circulation. They are not yet claims of final architectural quality, material resolution, furniture specification, accessibility compliance, or stakeholder approval.
